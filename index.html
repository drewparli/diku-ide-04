Visualizing the Spectrum

This assignment asked us to look at visualizing a dataset composed of hands and the principal component analysis (PCA) that had been performed. In very basic terms, PCA takes a dataset of high dimensionality in one coordinate system (or in linear algebra terms, a base) and looks for another coordinate system that can more effectively describe the variation in the data. This means that if we sort the principal components by the amount of variance in the dataset they describe, then what we typically call the first principal component will explain the most variance. Likewise, the second principal component will explain the second most variance. The outlines to the left and the scatter plot below it can help us better understand this notion graphically. Be sure to hover the mouse over each section as you are reading so that the example graphic can be displayed.

The First Component

Lets look at the first principal component. In terms of the scatter plot, we can think of it as being the distance in the x-axis. Therefore, if you are hovering with your mouse over this text, you will see two highlighted points. These points represent the two hands in the dataset furthest apart in terms of the first principal component. Not suprisingly, they also represent the two extremes that we could imagine. The light green outline shows a hand whose fingers are very close together, while the dark green outline shows a hand whose fingers are very far apart. Now we better understand what was stated earlier in terms of the principal components describing or explaining the variance in the dataset. So we can conclude that the first principal component describes to a large degree how far apart all the fingers of the hard are from each other.

The Second Component

Now lets look at the second principal component. In terms of the scatter plot, this can be thought of as the y-axis. Here, we can see that again, the two points furthest from each other in terms of the second principal component show two very different hands. We might conclude that to a larger degree, this component is describing the range of motion in the thumb, as well as the middle, ring and little fingers. To a less degree it is also picking up on smaller variations in the index finger.

This consisted of highlighting the two most extreme points for each component so that the reader could gain a more intuitive understanding of what each principal component was explaining in this dataset.

I added colouring of individual toggled data points and rectangular selection. Using unique colors for data points helps with comparison of smaller sets of data points which have interesting correlations and promotes a richer analysis. The colouring is handled via a global pool of available colours. We decided to include as varied hues as possible while also avoiding using the other highlighting colours we have, like red and grey. To save the viewer from dealing with large sets of selected points with very minor differences in colour between them, we only add color to the first 5 points which are selected - the rest of coloured black.

The selection code is inspired by the solution found in this gist. Certain choices made in their code need to be implemented to adapt the solution, but the logic is the same. On the end event, when a selection has been made, the selection window's boundaries are checked and the colouring algorithm from before is employed.
